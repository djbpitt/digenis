Irregular verbs in early East Slavic

General

Orthography

We use a normalized orthography that writes only я (not ѧ or ꙗ) and only у (not оу or ꙋ). We also write я, и, and ю (not а, ы, and у) after consonants created by velar palatalization or jotation: ч, ш, ж, щ, and ц.

In his chapters about morphology in How Russian came to be the way it is, Bloomington: Slavica, 2015, Tore Nesset usually provides the vernacular ESl forms of words even in situations where ESl scribes, at least in the earlier period, often favored SSl alternatives (see Appendix 3, Comparison of OCS and early Rusian, pp. 302–04 in Horace G. Lunt, The language of Rus′ in the eleventh century: some observations about facts and theories. Harvard Ukrainian studies 12/13 (1988), pp. 276–313. This supplement provides both forms where they differ. Our verb class numbering system is based on Nesset’s identification of five classes of regular verbs (137), which serves the starting point for the table below. Nesset says little about exceptions, though, and this site is intended to supplement the information about largely regular verbs in Nesset by addressing questions about verbs that do not fit neatly or regularly into the five classes.

Subtypes of basic verb classes

Nesset provides full paradigms for each of the five athematic (Class 5) verbs, and describes some of the principal subtypes in prose, and we focus here on subtypes and exceptions. The decision about what constitutes a subtype and what constitutes an exception is somewhat subjective, and here we regard verbs with different stem vowels in the present and past systems as irregular, and other instances where verbs with a class differ in their behavior as subtypes. The lists of subtypes and exceptions are not intended to be exhaustive; we have omitted patterns that are easily recognizable because of their similarity to modern Russian, as well as low-frequency types.

Class 1

Description

Class 1 verbs add all endings directly to the stem, e.g., нести, несуть carry.

Subtypes

Velar stems (b)

Stems that end in velar consonants exhibit regular consonant mutations in three contexts:

  • The first regressive palatalization occurs in most places where the stem-final velar consonant is followed by a front vowel, e.g., root /sěk-/ cut, 3sg present сѣчеть, 3sg aorist сѣче; cf. modern Russian 3sg present сечёт.

  • The second regressive palatalization occurs in the imperative because the endings go back to the diphthongs *oi, that is, were not originally front vowels (Nesset 235), e.g., сѣци, сѣцѣте. Furthermore, the root vowel -е- is replaced by -ь- in the imperative, e.g., infinitive речи (SSl рещи), 3sg present речеть, but imperative рьци, рьцѣте say.

  • The infinitive also shows a result identical to that of *dj palatalization, e.g., сѣчи (SSl сѣщи); cf. modern сечь. This is a regular development of *ktei even though the front vowel did not immediately follow the velar (Nesset 234).

Exceptions

  • Verbs with -ьн- or -ьм- in the present tense (e.g., начьну begin, възъму take) replace that sequence with я in the infinitive stem (e.g., начяти, възяти). This is a regular development of nasal diphthongs (*vъ-zьm-tei > въ-зя-ти; Nesset 205–07). Cf. modern начну, начать; возьму, взять.

  • Verbs with -ьр- replace that sequence with -ере- (SSl -рѣ-, ESl also writes -ре-) in the infinitive stem (e.g., 3pl present умьруть, infinitive умерети [SSl умрѣти]; cf. modern Russian умру, умереть). The verb 3pl отъвьрзуть open has either SSl infinitive отъврѣсти or отъвьрсти.
